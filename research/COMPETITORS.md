## 1. CleanShot X (cleanshot.com) — the general benchmark

- **Hero**: positions as the complete screen-capture toolkit for Mac. Headline spirit:
  "Capture your screen like a pro." Hero shows the actual capture UI / annotated
  screenshots, dark-ish premium presentation, real product imagery.
- **Features**: long, dense feature catalog (scrolling capture, annotation, cloud
  upload, GIF/video recording, OCR, pinned screenshots, self-timer). Presented as a
  grid/scroll of capability cards with screenshots. Breadth is the pitch.
- **Pricing**: one-time license (~$29 lifetime ballpark) plus a separate CleanShot
  Cloud subscription tier. Setapp distribution as well. (Verify exact numbers.)
- **Tone**: confident, premium, pro-creator. Polished but not playful.
- **Accent**: teal/blue-leaning brand with dark surfaces.
- **What to steal**: how they make breadth legible (many capabilities without chaos),
  the demo-first feature cards. **Where Quirky beats it**: CleanShot is capture+annotate;
  Quirky is capture+EXTRACT five data types. Different axis. Quirky should NOT try to
  match CleanShot's recording/annotation breadth; it should own "extraction."

## 2. Pixel Snap 3 (getpixelsnap.com) — direct rival to SPX mode

- **Hero**: measurement tool for designers. Spirit: "Measure anything on your screen."
  Shows on-screen measurement overlays with pixel readouts.
- **Features**: smart edge detection, measure distances, alignment guides, export.
  This is exactly Quirky's SPX mode, done as a whole product.
- **Pricing**: one-time (~$10-15 ballpark). Verify.
- **Tone**: utilitarian, designer-focused, clean.
- **Accent**: neutral with a measurement-cyan.
- **What to steal**: the visual grammar of on-screen measurement (the dimension labels,
  edge-detect snap lines) for Quirky's SPX feature cell. **Where Quirky beats it**:
  SPX is one of five Quirky modes; a Pixel Snap user pays for measurement alone, a
  Quirky user gets measurement plus four more behind the same hotkey.

## 3. Sip (sipapp.io) — rival to HEX mode

- **Hero**: a beautiful color picker for Mac. Spirit: "The best way to collect, organize,
  and share your colors." Soft, designerly, palette-forward presentation.
- **Features**: pick any pixel, palettes, color formats/export, team palette sync,
  global shortcut.
- **Pricing**: subscription (~$10/yr ballpark) plus free tier. Verify.
- **Tone**: warm-ish, design-craft, tasteful. Closest in mood to where Quirky wants to
  sit, useful warmth reference.
- **Accent**: relatively neutral so colors can be the star.
- **What to steal**: the elegance of color presentation (swatch + format), the calm
  designerly warmth. **Where Quirky beats it**: HEX is one tap of Tab away from four
  other extractions; Sip only does color.

## 4. ColorSlurp (colorslurp.com) — rival to HEX mode

- **Hero**: free color picker / eyedropper / palettes. Spirit: a friendly, generous
  color toolkit. More playful and colorful than Sip.
- **Features**: magnifier eyedropper, color formats, palettes, contrast checker, free
  with Pro upgrade.
- **Pricing**: freemium, Pro one-time/subscription. Verify.
- **Tone**: friendly, approachable, a little playful, multicolor brand. A friendliness
  reference, but Quirky must avoid multicolor (one-accent doctrine).
- **What to steal**: the friendly, generous tone. **Avoid**: its multicolor identity
  (Quirky is single-accent). **Where Quirky beats it**: breadth beyond color.

## 5. TextSniper (textsniper.app) — rival to OCR mode

- **Hero**: grab text from anything on screen. Spirit: "Copy text from images, videos,
  and more." Clear single-purpose OCR pitch.
- **Features**: on-screen OCR, QR/barcode read, speak-selection, works offline.
- **Pricing**: one-time (~$7-10) plus Setapp. Verify.
- **Tone**: simple, direct, utilitarian.
- **What to steal**: the crisp single-job clarity ("grab text from anything"), the
  "works offline" trust line. **Where Quirky beats it**: OCR plus four more modes.

## 6. Raycast (raycast.com) — premium menu-bar vibe + multi-capability model

- **Hero**: launcher for everything. Spirit: "Your shortcut to everything" /
  "Do more, faster." Hero shows the command palette UI live, dark theme, dense.
- **Features**: extensions store, AI, snippets, clipboard history, window management,
  presented as a deep, modular ecosystem.
- **Pricing**: free for individuals, Pro (~$8/mo) with AI, Team/Org tiers. Shows both
  direct and (historically) does NOT hide platform paths. Verify.
- **Tone**: confident, dev-premium, slightly cool. Dark-first.
- **Accent**: red/coral mark on dark surfaces.
- **What to steal**: how they present MANY capabilities under one launcher without
  overwhelming (compositional clarity), and how they make a menu-bar/keyboard utility
  feel premium. The "one entry point, many powers" framing maps directly onto Quirky's
  "one hotkey, five modes." **Diverge from**: dark-first theme and cool tone; Quirky is
  warm-light and friendly. **Don't copy**: extension-ecosystem framing (Quirky has no
  ecosystem; honesty rule).

## 7. Linear (linear.app) — craft/typography/motion benchmark (not a feature rival)

- **Hero**: project tracking for software teams. Spirit: "Build what matters" /
  "The issue tracker you'll enjoy using." Live product UI, near-monochrome, indigo
  accent, restrained scroll motion.
- **Features**: dense, hairline bento grids, sticky-column feature scroll, very tight
  typography.
- **Pricing**: free / standard / plus, per-seat. Not relevant to Quirky's model.
- **Tone**: precise, premium, minimal. The discipline benchmark.
- **What to steal**: typographic density, hairline grids, restrained scroll motion,
  the way borders carry the layout. **Diverge from**: the noir/indigo coolness;
  Quirky takes Linear's DISCIPLINE and pours warmth and blobs over it.

## Synthesis: the gap Quirky owns

Every direct rival is a **single-purpose** tool (Sip/ColorSlurp = color, TextSniper =
text, Pixel Snap = measure). CleanShot is broad but on a different axis (capture +
annotate + share, not extract-as-data). Raycast is broad but is a launcher, not a
screen-extractor. **No one occupies "one screen capture, five kinds of structured data
out."** That is Quirky's whole position, and it should be stated literally and shown
in the hero (the Capture Fan, see quirky-direction.md).

Pricing pattern across the field: solo Mac utilities sit at one-time ~$7-29 or light
subscription ~$3-10/mo, often with Setapp distribution. Quirky's model is LOCKED
(product-truth.md): **one-time unlock, Free core + Pro one-time ~$16.99, no
subscription.** This sits squarely in the field's one-time band and undercuts the
"buy three single-purpose tools" stack. The competitive anchor copy should be
"consolidation value of five single-purpose tools in one" (Sip + TextSniper + Pixel
Snap bought separately already exceeds a single Quirky license), plus the friendly
frame "a friend does not send you a monthly bill" (one-time vs CleanShot's
update-treadmill and Raycast/Sip subscriptions is a genuine, honest differentiator).

## Non-obvious observations (creative angles competitors miss)

1. **No rival sells the "Tab to switch" gesture as a hero moment.** The mode-switch
   mid-capture is genuinely novel and toy-delightful. Competitors can't show it because
   they only have one mode. Quirky should make Tab-switching a visible, animated star,
   not a footnote.
2. **The "five separate subscriptions you already pay for" frame is unused and strong.**
   A designer might literally pay Sip + Pixel Snap + a text tool. Quirky can show that
   stack collapsing into one (honest, concrete, on-budget to illustrate with blobs).
3. **All color rivals lead with palettes/organization; none lead with "grab the hex of
   the exact pixel under your cursor right now."** Quirky's HEX should stay in-the-moment,
   not become a palette manager (scope discipline).
4. **DOM/SVG extraction has no consumer-Mac competitor at all** (it lives in browser
   DevTools/extensions). Quirky has a clear, uncontested differentiator here for the
   frontend segment. Feature it prominently for that audience.
